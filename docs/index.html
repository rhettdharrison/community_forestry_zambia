Community Forest Management in Zambia: Is It Working?

DRAFT REPORT

Author

Rhett D Harrison

Published

March 16, 2026

This report presents findings from a survey of Community Forest Management Groups (CFMGs) across Zambia, assessing the effectiveness of community-based forest management and identifying opportunities for improved implementation.

Summary

Community forest management (CFM) has expanded rapidly in Zambia. As of 2025, 351 Community Forest Management Groups (CFMGs) preside over approximately 9.4 million hectares of community forests across all ten provinces. This report presents findings from a survey of 75 CFMGs across eight provinces, drawing on focus group discussions (FGDs) with CFMG executives and community female, male and youth groups, and individual interviews with community members, traditional leaders, District Forestry Officers (DFOs) and NGO representatives. The survey was designed to assess whether CFM is achieving its intended outcomes of improved sustainable forest management and rural development, and to identify where improvements are needed.

The headline finding is that CFM is broadly working, but with significant and addressable weaknesses.

In terms of forest management, the evidence is encouraging. Clear majorities of community groups report that CFM has reduced deforestation, lowered fire frequency and increased wildlife populations inside their community forests — the three most direct indicators of sustainable forest management. Importantly, most groups also report that deforestation is decreasing in areas outside their community forests, suggesting that CFM is not simply displacing pressure elsewhere. Conservation NGOs and carbon trading enterprises have been the primary drivers of these improvements, funding forest patrols, fire management, and agricultural extension for climate-smart agriculture across the communities they support.

However, the governance foundations underpinning these outcomes are fragile. Many CFMGs have not completed the legal processes required to formalise their status, including registration certificates, forest-use permits and benefit-sharing agreements. Basic financial management infrastructure — bank accounts, receipt books, financial reporting — is absent in a large proportion of CFMGs. Most are overdue for elections and in breach of their own bylaws. Business plans, annual workplans and budgets are lacking in many cases. These are not peripheral concerns: without sound governance, CFMGs cannot manage finances, account for income, or sustain the improvements in forest management that have been achieved. The Forestry Department has a statutory duty to support and oversee CFMGs, but DFO visits are infrequent across most of the country, leaving many CFMGs without meaningful state support. Where NGOs and carbon enterprises are present they partly fill this gap, but coverage is uneven and a substantial number of CFMGs receive no external support at all.

The economic outcomes of CFM remain limited and unevenly distributed. Approximately one third of CFMGs had received carbon payments at the time of the survey — a significant achievement, but one that leaves two thirds with little tangible financial return. Employment has grown under CFM, but is concentrated in low-paying roles such as Honorary Forest Officers, is strongly male-dominated, and is absent or minimal in many communities. Beyond carbon and honey — the only forest commodity exploited by more than a small minority of CFMGs — the vast potential of Zambia’s forest products as a basis for community enterprise is almost entirely unrealised. Timber, wild fruits, medicinal plants, mushrooms, bamboo and resins are all harvested from community forests, but overwhelmingly for household use or local sale with minimal value-addition. CFM has the potential to be a platform for diverse, layered forest enterprise: that potential is not yet being captured.

Equity outcomes are broadly positive. Most female FGD groups reported that benefits are shared equitably, that women benefit from CFM, and that women participate in decision making — a more positive picture than might be expected given that natural resource management in Zambia has traditionally been male-dominated. The formal governance structures mandated by CFM, including elections and executive positions with female representation, appear to have given women a degree of voice they previously lacked. However, a minority of female and youth groups identified problems with elite capture, lack of transparency, and exclusion from decision making. Youth groups were consistently the most critical of CFM outcomes across all domains. These are warning signals that must be taken seriously: elite capture and inequitable benefit distribution, if left unchecked, will erode community support for CFM and undermine its long-term viability.

Communities themselves are clear-eyed about both the strengths and the gaps in CFM. Their feedback on what is working — forest management, wildlife recovery, some livelihood improvements — matches the quantitative findings. Their identification of what needs improving — transparency, governance, community awareness, livelihoods and partnerships — provides a practical agenda for action. Their training needs, spanning fire management and conservation through to financial management, marketing and business development, reflect the full scope of what effective CFM demands of community members.

In short, CFM in Zambia has achieved real gains in forest management and, in many communities, in livelihoods and equity. Those gains are at risk if governance weaknesses are not addressed, if the Forestry Department does not fulfil its oversight role, and if economic development remains narrowly dependent on carbon. The opportunity to build on what is working — to formalise what has been achieved, diversify the economic base, and extend effective support to the many CFMGs currently struggling — is substantial. Detailed recommendations for government, NGOs and carbon trading enterprises are provided in the Recommendations chapter.
